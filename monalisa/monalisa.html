Story & Origin

The Mona Lisa is arguably the most famous painting in the world, created by the renowned Renaissance artist Leonardo da Vinci. The subject is widely believed to be Lisa Gherardini, the wife of Florentine merchant Francesco del Giocondo, which is why the painting is also known as La Gioconda. The work was likely commissioned to celebrate the birth of their second son or the purchase of their new home.

Leonardo began the portrait in Florence around 1503 and continued working on it for several years, taking it with him when he moved to France in 1516. He kept the painting until his death in 1519, after which it was acquired by King Francis I of France. The Mona Lisa became part of the French royal collection and eventually found its home in the Louvre Museum after the French Revolution.

The painting is renowned for its technical innovations, particularly Leonardo's use of sfumato—a technique of blending colors and tones so subtly that they appear to melt into one another. This creates the famous ambiguous smile that seems to change depending on the viewer's perspective. The landscape background, with its hazy mountains and winding rivers, demonstrates Leonardo's mastery of atmospheric perspective.

The Mona Lisa's fame skyrocketed in 1911 when it was stolen from the Louvre by Vincenzo Peruggia, an Italian patriot who believed the painting should be returned to Italy. The theft made international headlines, and the painting's recovery two years later turned it into a global icon. During its absence, more people went to see the empty space where it had hung than had visited the painting in the previous 12 years.

Today, the Mona Lisa is displayed in a climate-controlled, bulletproof glass case in the Louvre's Salle des États. It attracts millions of visitors annually, who often find themselves surprised by its relatively small size. The painting has been the subject of extensive scientific analysis, conservation efforts, and countless interpretations, securing its status as an enduring symbol of Western art and human creativity.
